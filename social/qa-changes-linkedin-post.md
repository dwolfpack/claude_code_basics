QA used to mean writing test cases and running regressions. That job is disappearing.

Not because quality doesn't matter.

Because AI is now writing the tests.

I've watched teams cut test-writing time by 70% with AI-assisted generation. Scripts that used to take days — done in hours. Regression suites that took weeks to maintain — refreshed in an afternoon.

But here's what I didn't expect:

The QA engineers who struggled weren't the slow ones. They were the ones who only knew how to execute — not how to think about quality.

The ones thriving now ask different questions:

→ What are we NOT testing that could hurt us?
→ Are we testing the right behaviour, or just the happy path?
→ What would a real user do that we've never planned for?

The role isn't shrinking. It's shifting.

From test writers → quality architects.
From execution → strategy.

The moat isn't Selenium skills anymore.

It's the instinct for what breaks, why it matters, and how to prevent it before a single line of code is written.

That's the kind of thinking AI can't replace.
